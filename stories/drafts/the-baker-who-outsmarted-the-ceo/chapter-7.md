# Chapter 7 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

Priya's article ran Sunday at 6 a.m. on *The Coastal Record*, a regional digital publication with a food section that was, Maya now understood, exactly where Priya had been building toward this whole time. The headline was: **A Recipe for Theft: How Holt Foods Built Its "Artisan" Brand on Stolen Heritage.**

By 8 a.m. it had been shared four hundred times. By noon, a national food trade publication had picked it up. By mid-afternoon, two local TV stations had called the bakery.

Maya did not answer the stations. She did answer the call from her attorney, David Osei, who had reviewed the USPTO filing and the food scientist's analysis and told her that she had, in his professional opinion, a strong infringement case. She also answered the call from Jamie, who was monitoring the bakery's online presence from his apartment and reporting in like a field correspondent.

"The *Table & Street* article has been taken down," he said.

"How long did that take?"

"About four hours after Priya's piece went live."

"Good."

"Also — someone posted the original Chen's Hearth formula that Priya included in the article, alongside a side-by-side analysis a food blogger did comparing it to the Holt sourdough. It's been shared two thousand times and counting."

Maya had not known Priya would include the formula. She had given Priya the trademark documentation, not the recipe itself. She called Priya.

"I included the method, not the exact formula," Priya said. "The difference matters legally. But I needed people to see what was being protected. I wanted it to be undeniable."

It was undeniable.

Derek Holt did not call Sunday. He called Monday at 8:15 a.m., and when Maya answered, David Osei was standing in her kitchen with his coffee and his briefcase, and she had him on speaker before Derek had finished his first sentence.

"Maya." Derek's voice was measured. The warmth was gone, the practiced ease stripped back to something cooler and more functional. "I'd like to meet with you before this gets further out of hand."

"I think it's already out of hand," Maya said. "I have an attorney here. Anything you want to say to me you can say to both of us."

A beat. "We'd like to resolve this quietly."

David said, "What terms are you proposing?"

Another beat, longer. Derek had not expected an attorney. "I'd want to discuss that in person."

"We're available Wednesday at two," David said. "Our office."

When she hung up, she felt something she had not felt in weeks: the particular satisfaction of a fermentation that has finally turned. Not triumph — not yet — but the knowledge that the process was working. That patience and heat and the right conditions were doing what they were supposed to do.

The city food expo ran Tuesday through Thursday at the convention center downtown. Holt Foods had a booth the size of a small house — Maya had seen the floor plan published in the event guide. The Artisan Collection launch event, with media and buyers and sampling stations, was scheduled for Tuesday evening.

She had not planned to go. David had told her to keep a low profile until Wednesday's meeting.

Then Jamie texted at noon Tuesday: *They haven't pulled the products. Holt Artisan booth is set up and they're doing final prep. Sesame rounds, heritage rye, sourdough — all there. I can see from the main floor.*

She called David.

"They haven't withdrawn the products," she said.

"No. They're betting the meeting Wednesday reshapes the narrative before any legal action is filed. Standard strategy — get to the table, offer terms, slow-walk the settlement."

"If they launch tonight, those products go into buyer orders. Distribution contracts. It becomes much harder to—"

"Yes," David said. "It does."

Maya thought for thirty seconds. "I'm going to the expo."

"Maya—"

"Not to make a scene. I'm going to be present. With Priya. In public. So that any buyer or journalist who is at that launch event can see that the person whose grandmother's formula is on the table is standing thirty feet away and is not invisible."

David was quiet. "That's not legally actionable in itself," he said.

"No," Maya said. "It isn't."

She put on the cleanest apron she owned — white, with *Chen's Hearth* embroidered on the chest in the font her grandmother had chosen — and she called Priya and she went to the expo.

The Holt booth was everything corporate ambition could make it: warm lighting, handsome signage, sample trays laid out with the careful informality of something designed to look uncontrived. The sourdough was sliced and fanned on wooden boards. The packaging faced outward. *Heritage method. Small-batch. Artisan-crafted.*

Maya walked to the edge of the booth and stood there.

She didn't say anything. She didn't need to. Priya was beside her with her notebook, identifiable to anyone who'd read Sunday's article. A food blogger Maya didn't know recognized her and came over. Then a buyer from a co-op grocery chain. Then a journalist from the trade publication that had picked up Priya's story.

Derek saw her from across the booth floor. She watched him see her. She watched the calculation play across his face — the weighing of options, the consideration of responses — and she kept her expression neutral and her hands still at her sides.

Claire Voss materialized at Derek's elbow and said something to him too quietly to hear. He nodded. He looked at the sample trays. He looked at Maya.

He said something to Claire. She disappeared.

Two minutes later, a Holt Foods staff member began quietly, efficiently removing the sourdough sample trays from the display. Then the rye. Then the sesame rounds. Not all the products — the booth continued — but the three that were named in Priya's article and David Osei's preliminary infringement notice, which had been filed that morning and which Derek Holt certainly knew about.

Priya was writing in her notebook. The co-op buyer was watching. The trade journalist had taken out his phone.

Maya didn't smile. She turned to Priya. "I need to get back," she said. "I have bread in the oven."

"Of course you do," Priya said.

They walked out. Behind them, the expo floor buzzed with exactly the kind of conversation Derek Holt had not wanted to have on his launch night.
