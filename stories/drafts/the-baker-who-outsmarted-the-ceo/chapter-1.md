# Chapter 1 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The alarm never needed to go off. Maya Chen was already awake at 3:47 a.m., sitting at the edge of her bed in the dark, running through the day's bakes in her head the way other people run through their worries.

Starter fed. Levain ready by five. The rosemary-olive loaves first because they took the longest. Then the cardamom morning buns. Then whatever she felt like making, which today was going to be the black sesame sourdough she'd been refining for three weeks — a recipe she'd built from memory and instinct and her grandmother's handwritten notes, which were written half in English and half in Cantonese and smelled faintly of vanilla even now, twenty years after Nai Nai had set down her pen for the last time.

Maya got up and padded through the dark apartment above the bakery. The stairs creaked in their usual spots — third step, seventh step, the landing. She could walk them blind, which she often did.

Chen's Hearth occupied the ground floor of a narrow three-story building on Fulton Street in the Riverside District, a neighborhood that the city's real estate listings had been calling "transitional" for the past six years, which was their polite way of saying it was still mostly working people and corner stores and excellent food that nobody downtown had bothered to photograph yet. The bakery had been there for thirty-one years. Maya's grandmother Lily Chen had opened it with her husband Thomas in the spring of their second year in the country, using a borrowed stand mixer, a secondhand deck oven, and a sourdough starter she had carried in a glass jar from Hong Kong.

Thomas was gone now, fifteen years buried. Lily was gone four years ago. Maya had inherited everything: the building, the starter, the recipes, the regulars, and the debt on a refrigeration unit that had since been paid off and replaced.

She didn't think of herself as a businesswoman. She thought of herself as a baker. The distinction mattered to her in a way she couldn't always explain. Businesswomen thought about margins and positioning and brand voice. Bakers thought about fermentation times and crust color and whether the crumb on today's loaf was open enough. Maya was thirty-four years old and had spent most of her adult life in this kitchen, and she was, by every measure that mattered to her, exactly where she was supposed to be.

By 4:15 she had the ovens on and the first levain weighed out. By 4:30 Jamie Park had arrived — she heard his key in the back door and then his sneakers on the tile and then his voice, which he used quietly this early because he understood that the morning had its own rhythm and you didn't barge into it.

"Starter smells good," he said, hanging his jacket on the hook.

"Three days of extra feeds," Maya said. "It's very happy right now."

Jamie was twenty-two, recently graduated from a culinary program he'd found insufficiently obsessive, and had showed up at the bakery fourteen months ago asking not for a job but to apprentice. Maya had said she didn't do apprenticeships. He'd said he'd work for free for a week to prove he was serious. She'd said that was illegal. He'd said he'd work for minimum wage then, whatever, he just wanted to learn how to make bread like hers. She'd hired him the next Monday and had not regretted it once.

They worked in companionable silence for the first hour. The rosemary-olive loaves went in at five-fifteen. The cardamom buns were shaped and proofing in the cold case by five-forty. The black sesame sourdough — Maya's current obsession — was scored at six and loaded just before the first regulars began arriving at seven.

The regulars were a specific cast. Mr. Okafor from the dry cleaner two blocks over, who bought one rye loaf every Tuesday and Friday without exception and had for nine years. Rosa Delgado from next door, who came in every morning for a coffee and whatever Maya thought she needed that day, which Rosa accepted without complaint as a form of care. The young couple from the apartment building across the street whose baby had been born six months ago and who now came in every morning looking like they needed the bread more than they needed air. Three teenagers from the high school who had discovered the morning buns and now showed up precisely when the case was stocked, every day, with a loyalty that bordered on religious.

This was Maya's world. It was small and warm and smelled of fermented grain and browning butter, and she was, genuinely, happy in it.

Which was why the man in the linen blazer struck her wrong the moment he walked in.

He didn't look at the bread. Most people, even people who didn't know anything about bread, looked at the bread when they walked in. The display case ran the full length of the front counter and the loaves on the shelves behind it were always arranged by Jamie with a care that bordered on ceremony. People looked. This man looked at her.

He was maybe fifty, with the kind of grooming that requires a standing appointment somewhere. Silver at the temples. A watch that Maya's ex-boyfriend, who had been briefly into watches, would have identified immediately. He stood just inside the door and scanned the room the way people scan rooms when they're making a calculation, not a purchase.

"Maya Chen?" he said.

"That depends on why you're asking."

A smile. Practiced, not warm. "Derek Holt. I run Holt Foods." He said it the way people say the name of a place they expect you to recognize, with a slight pause after, waiting for the flicker of acknowledgment. Maya gave him nothing. She'd heard of Holt Foods — they had a chain of upscale grocery stores across the state and had recently launched something called the Holt Artisan Collection, which she'd seen in a display at the airport, little kraft-paper packages with serif fonts and words like *small-batch* and *heritage method* printed on them. She had thought, at the time, that they smelled like a food scientist's idea of a bakery.

"What can I get you?" she said.

He pulled an envelope from the inside pocket of his blazer and set it on the counter. "I'd like to talk to you about this building."

Maya looked at the envelope. Then at him. "I'm not selling."

"You haven't opened it."

"I don't need to. This building belonged to my grandmother. I live here and I work here and I'm not interested in selling it."

Derek Holt nodded slowly, in the way of a man who has been told no before and has developed a philosophy around it. "I understand the sentimental attachment. Truly. But the Riverside District is changing. The city has approved a development corridor that runs right through this block. You're going to have opportunities you don't have right now, and some pressures you don't currently have either, and I'd hate to see you navigate that without knowing your options."

Maya slid the envelope back across the counter toward him. "Rye loaf? Cardamom bun? The black sesame is out of the oven in about twenty minutes and it's going to be worth the wait."

He looked at the envelope on the counter between them for a long moment. Then he picked it up. "Think about it," he said. "The offer is generous. I'm not here to pressure you."

He left. He had not bought anything.

Jamie appeared at Maya's elbow. He'd been pretending to organize the pastry case. "Who was that?"

"A man who doesn't look at bread," Maya said.

"Bad sign?"

Maya watched Derek Holt's linen blazer disappear past the window. She thought about the envelope. She thought about the phrase *development corridor*, which she had not liked the sound of at all.

"Yeah," she said. "Bad sign."

The black sesame sourdough came out of the oven eleven minutes later, its scored surface a deep, glossy brown, its crust crackling softly as it cooled on the wire rack. Maya pressed her ear against it — a habit, a ritual, something her grandmother had done — and listened to it sing.

Whatever was coming, the bread was good. It was always good. That was the one thing she could count on.
