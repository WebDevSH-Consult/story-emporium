# Chapter 2 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The health inspector arrived on a Thursday, which was Maya's busiest day. She knew the inspector — a compact, apologetic man named Gerald Marsh who had been doing the rounds in the Riverside District for years and who had never once given Chen's Hearth anything worse than a minor note about a grease trap. He came in through the front, which was unusual. Inspectors usually came through the back.

He wasn't alone. The woman with him wore a badge Maya didn't recognize: city seal, different department. She had a clipboard and was already looking at things.

"Maya," Gerald said, and he had the careful tone of someone delivering news they'd been handed, not news they'd found themselves. "I need to do a full inspection today. Full documentation."

"Sure," Maya said. "You know where everything is."

It took two hours. At the end of it, Gerald presented her with a three-page citation form. Maya read through it slowly, standing at the prep counter while Jamie watched from across the kitchen with his arms folded.

The citations were real in the technical sense. A chip in one of the floor tiles near the back door, which had been chipped for eleven years and had never previously been noted. A certificate for the deck oven's last service that was four months past its renewal date — the service had been done, but Maya had not yet received the updated paperwork from the technician. A temperature log for the cold case that had two days of missing entries because Jamie had been sick and she'd forgotten to keep up with the paper record even though the unit had been running perfectly.

All of it was real. None of it was dangerous. All of it was suddenly documented.

"Gerald," Maya said quietly. "This is four months of work to fix on paper."

"I know." He didn't meet her eyes. "I'm sorry, Maya."

The woman from the other department photographed every page. She was gone before Maya finished reading.

That evening, Rosa Delgado came around through the alley door the way she'd been doing for thirty years. Rosa was seventy-one, short and solid, with white hair she kept in a braid and the bearing of a woman who had outlasted several people who had tried to make her feel small. She sat on the stool by the cooling rack and watched Maya clean the deck oven.

"You going to tell me what happened today?" Rosa said.

"Health inspection. Citations."

Rosa was quiet for a moment. "First time in eleven years."

"First time in eleven years."

"Man came by in the morning, I saw him from my window. Fancy blazer. He's been here before — maybe two weeks ago, I saw him walk the block."

Maya stopped scrubbing. "Derek Holt."

Rosa said nothing. She didn't need to. In the Riverside District, they had all seen this movie before. A developer decides he wants a block. He gets the block — by hook or by complication or by making the paperwork so heavy that small owners simply sink. The citations were the first move. The building pressure would come next.

It came three days later in the form of a letter from Maya's landlord — not her own building, which she owned, but the shared parking agreement with the lot behind her block that she paid a small monthly fee for. The letter said the agreement would not be renewed when it expired in ninety days. The lot had been purchased by a development group. The letter listed an address Maya didn't recognize, but Jamie Googled it in thirty seconds and found it traced back to a holding company with one registered agent: Holt Development Partners LLC.

"He bought the parking lot," Jamie said, staring at his phone.

"He bought the parking lot," Maya confirmed.

"Can he do that?"

"He did it." Maya read the letter a second time. The parking wasn't essential — she didn't have customers who drove, mostly — but the delivery access was. Her flour supplier, her dairy distributor, both came through that lot. Without it, deliveries would have to come around to Fulton Street, which was narrow and metered and would cost her real money every week.

The third move came in the form of a comment piece published on a local food and lifestyle website called *Table & Street*. The piece was titled "Is the Riverside District Ready for a Real Food Scene?" and it included, in its third paragraph, a brief mention of Chen's Hearth as "a neighborhood stalwart with loyal regulars but limited hours and a menu that hasn't changed much in decades — charming, perhaps, but not indicative of the district's potential."

The writer's bio listed no affiliation, but Priya Nair, who freelanced for three publications Maya actually respected, left a comment below the piece that said: *This is lazy framing. The bakery in question has some of the most technically accomplished sourdough in the city. Who briefed you on this?*

Maya read the comment at midnight, sitting alone at the prep counter with the lights low. She saved Priya's name in her phone under no particular label, but she didn't delete it.

She thought about her grandmother. Lily Chen had also been here when the neighborhood went through a rough cycle in the nineties. She'd kept the bakery open through it. When Maya had asked her, years later, how she'd done it, Lily had said something Maya still kept close: *I knew what I was making and I knew who it was for. Nobody can take that from you. They can make it harder but they cannot take it.*

Maya fed the starter at one in the morning, the way she always did. The culture was sixty-three years old, carried across an ocean in a glass jar. It had survived a lot.

She was going to need it to survive a little more.
