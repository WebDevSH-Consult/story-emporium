# Chapter 5 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The filing cabinet lived in the back room behind the proofing shelves. It was a heavy, olive-green thing from the eighties that Maya had never once considered throwing out because her grandmother had said it was not to be thrown out, and that had been sufficient. It held tax records, insurance documents, the original lease for the building's commercial space, and three folders labeled in Lily Chen's handwriting that Maya had never needed to open.

She opened them now.

The first held correspondence with a lawyer from 1998, mostly about the building purchase. The second held receipts for equipment going back to 1994. The third — labeled simply *L* — held a single document Maya had never seen before.

It was a United States Patent and Trademark Office registration. Dated March 14, 2001. Registered to Lily Mei Chen, doing business as Chen's Hearth.

The trademarked item was listed as: *The Chen's Hearth Sourdough Starter Formula and Associated Long-Fermentation Method, including the specific composition, feeding schedule, and production process as detailed in the attached technical description.*

Maya sat down on the floor. She was shaking.

Her grandmother had trademarked the starter. The formula. The method. Twenty-five years ago, on a date that meant nothing to Maya except that it was six months after Maya's grandfather Thomas had died, which meant Lily had done it alone, quietly, without telling anyone — including, apparently, her granddaughter — and had simply filed it away in the olive-green cabinet behind the proofing shelves.

Maya read the document three times. The technical description attached ran to four pages. It described the starter's composition with a specificity that could only have come from someone who understood both the baking and the law — a starter carrying specific microbial ratios that Lily had cultivated over decades, a feeding schedule tied to temperature ranges, a production method involving timing intervals that Lily had refined across thirty years of daily practice.

She called Rosa.

Rosa was at the door within five minutes, still in her house slippers, and read the document in silence, standing at the prep counter.

"She never told you," Rosa said.

"She never told me."

Rosa set the document down. She looked, for a moment, like she was going to cry, but she didn't. "She told me," she said. "Once. Years ago. She said she'd done something to protect the bread. She said it in Cantonese and I only understood half of it, so I assumed she meant she'd written the recipes down somewhere." Rosa paused. "She was smarter than both of us."

Maya called Priya. Then she called the number on the patent registration for Lily's original attorney — a firm downtown — and left a message for whoever was still there. Then she sat with the document spread on the table and made herself think clearly.

The Holt Artisan Collection sourdough had launched four months ago. It had been in production, meaning in recipe development and testing, for at least six months before that — probably more. Someone on Derek Holt's team had developed it based on something. Maya had tasted it. It wasn't her grandmother's bread, not exactly, but the method had the same fingerprints: the extended ferment, the specific hydration, the secondary rise timing. If they had sampled Chen's Hearth sourdough closely — which anyone could do, by buying a loaf — and reverse-engineered it using the patented method as a template, they had been infringing for the better part of a year.

Priya arrived that afternoon with a notebook and a recorder and sat across from Maya at the corner table.

"Walk me through everything from the beginning," Priya said. "Everything you know and everything you can prove. We separate those two piles first."

What Maya knew: Derek Holt had been systematically targeting small bakeries in the region, extracting recipes through sampling and consultation, and scaling modified versions under the Artisan Collection brand. What she could prove: the taste similarities she'd documented, the timelines of closure, the holding company that owned the parking lot.

What she now additionally knew: her grandmother's formula was trademarked. What she could prove: a registered USPTO document in her hand.

What she needed to establish: that the Holt Artisan sourdough was derived from the Chen's Hearth method closely enough to constitute infringement. That was the harder part, and it required an expert.

"I know someone," Priya said. "A food scientist who consults on IP cases. She's analyzed Holt's products before, for a different matter that didn't go anywhere. She'll know what to look for."

Maya nodded. "How long do I have before the week is up and Derek expects an answer?"

"Three days."

"Can you get me the analysis in three days?"

Priya looked at her steadily. "I can get you enough. Are you ready to be on the record?"

Maya thought about her grandmother in the back room of a notary's office in 2001, thirty days after burying her husband, registering a trademark for a sourdough starter because she understood, in some quiet and absolute way, that the bread was worth protecting.

"Yeah," Maya said. "I'm ready."

She went back to the cabinet. Behind the three folders she'd already opened, in the very back of the bottom drawer, she found one more thing: a photograph she'd never seen, printed on paper that had gone slightly yellow. Her grandmother and grandfather, standing outside Chen's Hearth, the sign new and bright above them, the date written in Lily's hand on the back: *March 1992. First day.*

Maya set it on the counter where she could see it. Then she went to work.
