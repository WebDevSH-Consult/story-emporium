# Chapter 6 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The story appeared on a Friday morning.

It ran in *Table & Street* again — the same local lifestyle site that had published the dismissive piece two months earlier. The headline was: **Health Code Violations and Declining Sales: Is Chen's Hearth Past Its Prime?** The piece cited the health inspection citations by name, mentioned the parking situation, and included a quote from an unnamed "industry source" who described Maya's operation as "a sentimental institution running on fumes, resistant to growth and increasingly out of step with what the neighborhood needs."

Maya read it standing at the counter at 5 a.m., flour on her hands, starter already fed, loaves already in the oven.

Jamie read it over her shoulder. When he finished, he said nothing for a long moment. Then he said: "Unnamed industry source."

"Yeah."

"They don't even bother being subtle."

"They don't have to be," Maya said. "The citations are real. The parking is real. Anybody reading that who doesn't know this place is going to see a struggling business and a landlord dispute and a health department problem and draw their own conclusions." She put her phone face-down on the counter. "That's the point."

By nine o'clock, four of her regulars had come in and mentioned the article with varying degrees of outrage on her behalf, which meant it was circulating. By ten, three had told her they'd left comments defending the bakery. By noon, the *Table & Street* comment section had more activity than it had probably ever seen, including Priya Nair's second comment in two months on pieces about Chen's Hearth, this one longer: *This piece cites the bakery's health citations without noting that the citations were filed the week after the property owner declined a buyout offer from Holt Foods. Curious timing. More to come.*

Maya texted Priya: *Did you post that?*

Priya: *I did. I know it tips them off. But I needed them to know they're being watched before I publish.*

Maya: *When do you publish?*

Priya: *I'm waiting on one more source. The food scientist's analysis came back. It's good. Really good.*

Maya set the phone down and went back to work. There was nothing else to do. The bread still had to be made.

That afternoon, Councilwoman Sandra Torres stopped by the bakery for the first time in the three years she'd been representing the district. She was a tall woman in her late forties who wore her authority like clothing — always present, always fitted. She bought a loaf of the honey-wheat and accepted a coffee and sat at the corner table, and Maya sat across from her because she understood that this was not a social call.

"I wanted to check in," Torres said. "I heard about the article."

"Kind of you."

"And about the health inspection."

"Also kind."

Torres wrapped her hands around her coffee cup and looked at Maya with the careful expression of a politician preparing to say something true while leaving room to retreat from it. "Derek Holt has made some significant investments in this district's development plan. The corridor project is real, and it has real community benefits — new green space, updated infrastructure, improved transit access."

"For who?" Maya asked.

"For everyone."

"Including the people who are currently here?"

Torres didn't answer that directly. "I want to be helpful to you, Maya. I have real affection for this block, for what your grandmother built. But I also have to work with the reality of where the investment is coming from. If there's a middle ground here — a way to benefit from the development without losing what you've built—"

"He offered me a contract," Maya said. "I didn't sign it."

"I know."

"You know because he told you, or because you were part of the conversation?"

Torres's expression didn't change, but something tightened around her eyes. She stood, picked up her bread. "I hope you make the right decision," she said. "For yourself and for the neighborhood."

After she left, Rosa, who had been in the corner nursing a second coffee she hadn't needed for forty minutes, said, "She's in his pocket."

"Yeah."

"But she doesn't like being there." Rosa tilted her head. "Did you see her face when you asked if she was part of the conversation? She was embarrassed."

"People who are embarrassed can still do the wrong thing."

"Yes." Rosa finished her coffee. "But they sometimes get tired of it."

That evening Priya called. "I'm publishing Sunday morning," she said. "The food scientist's analysis confirms that three Holt Artisan products — the sourdough, the sesame rounds, and the heritage rye — use methods that are either directly derived from or substantially similar to trademarked formulas belonging to Chen's Hearth and two other now-closed local bakeries. I have four former operators on record. I have the USPTO filing. I have the holding company documentation for the parking lot. And I have—" she paused— "a former Holt Foods product developer who left the company eight months ago and is ready to talk about how the Artisan Collection was sourced."

Maya gripped the phone. "A whistleblower."

"She said she raised concerns internally and was told the legal team had 'reviewed the IP landscape.' She kept emails."

Maya stood at the counter in her kitchen, looking at the photograph of her grandparents on the first day. The bread in the oven behind her was already singing in the way bread does when it's right — that crackling, alive sound of a crust forming over something patient and warm.

"Okay," Maya said. "I'm ready. What do I need to do?"

"Sunday morning, the piece goes live. Monday morning, Derek Holt is going to call you. When he does, I want you to be standing next to an IP attorney."

"I know one," Maya said. "I called him two days ago."

Priya laughed — a short, bright sound. "I think you might be better at this than you think," she said.
