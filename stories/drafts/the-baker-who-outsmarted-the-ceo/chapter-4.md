# Chapter 4 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

Derek Holt came back on a Wednesday, and this time he brought a woman in a gray suit who introduced herself as Claire Voss, VP of Brand Development, Holt Foods. Claire had the kind of smile that was assembled rather than arrived at, and she carried a leather portfolio that probably cost more than Maya's first commercial mixer.

This time Derek did look at the bread. He walked the length of the case slowly, hands clasped behind his back, reading the small chalkboard labels Maya wrote out each morning in her grandmother's style — the name of the bread, the grain, the fermentation time.

"You write these yourself?" he said.

"Every day."

"That's charming." He said it genuinely, which was more unsettling than if he'd been condescending. "That's exactly the kind of detail we'd want to preserve."

Maya poured herself a coffee and did not offer them any. "You said you wanted to talk."

They sat at the small table in the corner, the one Maya used for inventory and her grandmother had used for everything. Claire opened the portfolio and laid a document on the table. It was thick — maybe fifteen pages — and the logo at the top read *Holt Foods Brand Partnership Agreement*.

"We've rethought our approach," Derek said. "Acquisition isn't the right word for what we're proposing. What we want is a partnership. You keep the building. You keep the bakery. We provide capital investment — equipment upgrades, facilities improvements, extended hours support — and in exchange we license the Chen's Hearth brand and your core recipes for the Artisan Collection."

Maya looked at the document without touching it. "You want to put Chen's Hearth on your packaging."

"We want to honor the legacy. Your grandmother's story, this building's history — that's exactly the kind of authentic provenance that our customers respond to. You'd be credited. Prominently."

Claire slid a one-page summary across the table. It outlined a licensing fee — a monthly figure that was, Maya had to admit, more than she made in a slow quarter. There were lines about "creative consultation rights" and "brand stewardship committee" and "recipe integrity preservation."

And at the bottom, in dense smaller text, was a section she almost skimmed past: *Licensor grants Holt Foods exclusive worldwide rights to the Licensed Recipes, including all derivatives and substantially similar formulations, in perpetuity.*

In perpetuity.

She read it again.

"The sourdough formula," she said. "The one my grandmother developed. The starter. If I sign this, you own it."

Derek leaned forward. "We'd be stewards of it. The starter stays with you — you'd still make the bread here. We'd be producing a version at scale, yes, but—"

"A version you own. Forever."

"Maya." His tone softened, and she recognized the gear shift — the place where negotiation becomes consolation. "The citations from the health department aren't going away. The parking situation is complicated. I'm not going to pretend the business environment is ideal for a small operator right now. This partnership would remove all of that. Clean slate. Real capital. Your name on a product that reaches three hundred thousand households."

She looked at the document. She thought about the floor tile with the chip in it and the missing temperature logs and the delivery drivers who had started calling to warn her that the lot was going to be locked in January. She thought about the refrigeration unit that had been making a sound she didn't entirely trust and the oven that needed a recalibration she'd been putting off.

She thought about the money. She was not going to lie to herself — she thought about the money.

"I'd want a lawyer to review it," she said.

"Of course." Derek sat back. "We can give you a week."

After they left, Jamie came out of the back. He'd been pretending to clean the mixer. "How bad is it?"

Maya folded the summary page and put it in her apron pocket. "It's structured so that it sounds like a lifeline and acts like a trap. The capital is real. The licensing terms would mean he owns Nai Nai's formula outright."

"You're not going to sign it."

"I don't know," Maya said, and the honesty of it surprised her. "I don't know yet."

She went upstairs that evening and sat at her grandmother's writing desk, the one she'd never moved or replaced, and laid the document out on it. She read every page. The creative consultation rights were non-binding. The brand stewardship committee met quarterly at Holt Foods' discretion. The recipe integrity preservation clause had a carve-out that allowed Holt Foods to modify recipes "for commercial production standards."

It was, she thought, a very elegant trap. Designed to look like an open door.

She called Priya.

"I have a contract in front of me," she said.

"Don't sign it," Priya said immediately.

"I haven't. But tell me something — the other operators you talked to. Did any of them get offered a partnership deal before things went bad?"

Silence on the line. Then: "Two of them. Both signed modified versions. Neither bakery exists anymore."

Maya stared at the document. Outside, Fulton Street was quiet, the way it always was at eleven on a weekday. She could hear, faintly, the sound of the starter working in its crock on the shelf below — that slow, living exhale she'd known her whole life.

"Okay," she said. "I need you to help me build something before the week is up. Something that makes it so I'm the one holding the cards when he comes back for my answer."

"What kind of something?"

Maya looked at the stack of bread wrappers she'd collected, still sitting on the kitchen counter. "The kind with documentation," she said.
