# Chapter 8 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The Wednesday meeting lasted four hours. David had told Maya it would take two. She had brought water and a tin of cardamom buns because she didn't know what else to do with her hands during negotiations and baking them at 5 a.m. had helped her think.

Derek Holt arrived with two attorneys, Claire Voss, and a man from Holt Foods' communications department whose job, Maya understood, was to manage the aftermath of whatever happened in the room. They sat on one side of a long table in David's conference room. Maya and David sat on the other. Priya was not there — she'd done her part, and what happened next was between parties and lawyers.

Derek opened by saying that Holt Foods had conducted an internal review and had concerns about their product development process in the early stages of the Artisan Collection. He said this with the tone of a man who had been coached, not contrite. He said that Holt Foods took intellectual property seriously and wanted to resolve any inadvertent overlaps.

David said the word *inadvertent* would need to leave the room before they made any progress.

Forty minutes later, it had.

The terms Maya and David had prepared were written out on four pages. She had read them so many times she could have recited them. Holt Foods would:

Cease production of the three products cited in the infringement notice. Remove them from all distribution and retail immediately. Issue a public acknowledgment — not quite an apology, but something with the shape of one — crediting the formula's origin to Chen's Hearth and its founder, Lily Chen. Pay a licensing fee, retroactive to the launch date, for the period of infringement. That figure was not small.

In addition — and this was the part Maya had written herself, in her own words, which David had told her was unusual for a legal settlement but had included without modification — Holt Foods would establish a Riverside District Artisan Fund: a five-year commitment of capital grants to small food producers in the neighborhoods where the Artisan Collection had sourced, without credit, from local operators. The fund would be administered independently. Holt Foods would have no say in who received the grants.

Derek's lead attorney pushed back on the fund for twenty minutes. Derek himself said nothing during that exchange. He was looking at a spot on the table near his water glass. Maya watched him and thought: *he knows exactly what he did, and he is going to sign this anyway because the alternative is a lawsuit that his communications man cannot contain.*

At 2:47 p.m., Derek Holt signed. His hand moved with the practiced certainty of a man who signed things often, but his jaw was tight.

Maya signed below him. Her hand was steady.

---

Priya's follow-up piece ran Thursday morning: **Holt Foods Settles IP Dispute with Chen's Hearth, Establishes Community Artisan Fund.** It was the most-shared piece *The Coastal Record* had published in two years. By Friday, two national food publications had picked it up. By the following Monday, a magazine Maya had never imagined reading her name in had emailed to ask about a profile.

She did not answer immediately. She was busy.

The bakery on the morning after the settlement was the same as the bakery on every other morning. The alarm didn't go off because Maya was already awake. The starter was fed. The levain was ready by five. Jamie arrived at 4:30, hung his jacket on the hook, and said that the starter smelled extremely good.

"It knows something's changed," Jamie said.

"It doesn't know anything," Maya said. "It's a microorganism."

"It's sixty-three years old. It's been through a lot." He looked at her. "So. What now?"

What now. Maya had thought about this. The settlement money was real. The citations had been quietly resolved — the health department had received a clarification memo, apparently, regarding the circumstances of the original inspection, and a follow-up visit by Gerald Marsh had resulted in a clean report and a small note in the file that bore no official label but communicated something. The parking situation was more complex, but Derek Holt's attorney had mentioned in passing that the development timeline for the lot had shifted, and David had told Maya to expect the issue to dissolve in the coming months.

What now was: the bakery. What it had always been.

Except that three weeks after the settlement, Rosa appeared at the alley door with a man Maya didn't recognize. He was in his seventies, with the build of someone who had spent decades lifting and carrying, and he carried a glass jar in both hands with the care people reserve for irreplaceable things.

"This is Andrzej," Rosa said.

The man with the rye starter — the rye that had ended up in Holt's packaging without his name on it. He had heard about the settlement and had called Rosa, who had given him Maya's number, who had said to come by.

He set the jar on the prep counter. "My mother's starter," he said. "From Kraków. Forty years old now. I have been keeping it since my shop closed but I am renting a very small apartment and I do not have room for an oven." He looked at Maya steadily. "Rosa says you are a person who understands what it means to keep something alive."

Maya looked at the jar. She thought about her grandmother in 1992, standing outside the bakery on the first day, holding a starter she had carried across an ocean.

"Yeah," Maya said. "I do."

She made space on the shelf beside Lily's crock. Two starters, side by side, both older than most of the businesses on the block.

By November, the Riverside District Artisan Fund had processed its first round of applications. Andrzej received a grant and signed a lease on a small commercial kitchen two blocks from Fulton Street. June, the farmer's market baker, applied and was approved for equipment upgrades. A young woman from Eastport named Nadia, whose family had run the Lebanese bakery, applied and was approved for a storefront deposit.

Councilwoman Torres voted yes on a zoning amendment that protected three blocks of small retail from mixed-use redevelopment. She did not explain her change of position publicly. She did not need to.

Priya's profile of Maya ran in January. The headline was: **The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO.** The photograph showed Maya at the counter at 5 a.m., flour on her apron, both starters visible on the shelf behind her. The caption identified Lily's crock and Andrzej's jar by name.

Maya read the piece at the prep counter before the ovens were hot, and when she was done she folded the magazine and put it next to the photograph of her grandparents on the first day, which she had moved from the back room to the counter where she could see it.

Then she got back to work.

The rosemary-olive loaves first. Then the cardamom buns. Then the black sesame sourdough, which she had finally stopped refining because she understood now that it was finished — that it was what it was going to be, and what it was going to be was very good.

Outside, Fulton Street was waking up. Mr. Okafor's dry cleaner sign came on. Three teenagers materialized on the corner at exactly the right time, the way they always did. Rosa came through the alley door and sat on the stool by the cooling rack and didn't say anything, because she didn't need to.

The bread was in the oven. It was going to be good.

It was always going to be good.
