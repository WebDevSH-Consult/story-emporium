# Chapter 3 - The Baker Who Outsmarted the CEO

The Holt Artisan Collection had launched four months ago in a splashy rollout that involved a rooftop event in the Arts District, a dozen food bloggers, and a press release that used the phrase *celebrating the heritage of local craft* four separate times. Maya had read about it with a mild, professional contempt and then forgotten about it.

She remembered it now.

It started with a customer — a woman named Dana who drove in from the suburbs every other Saturday for the rye loaf. Dana set her paper bag on the counter one morning in October and said, "I saw something in Holloway's last week that I almost bought instead of driving down here. Looked just like your rye. Same shape, same score pattern. They were calling it 'artisan heritage rye, small-batch, traditional long ferment.' I picked it up and something felt off. Then I thought, well, Maya's is better anyway, and I drove down."

Maya smiled and thanked her. Later, in the kitchen, she thought about it.

That weekend she went to the Holloway's Marketplace on Fifth — the flagship Holt Foods grocery, airy and expensive, with mood lighting over the produce and a whole wall of the Holt Artisan Collection products. She walked the bread section slowly.

The rye was there. Dark, dense, oblong, scored diagonally in threes. She bought it without a word and took it home.

She sliced it at the prep counter and tasted it. Then she was still for a moment.

It wasn't her rye. It wasn't even close to her rye, which was a relief. But the flavor profile — the balance of caraway and malt, the specific sour finish — was unmistakably a derivation of a rye she'd seen before. Not her bread. Rosa's neighbor, a Polish man named Andrzej who'd run a tiny deli on the next block before his lease was ended two years ago. Andrzej's rye had had exactly that balance, that finish. She'd once asked him how he achieved it and he'd said it was his mother's method, that she'd kept the starter going in Kraków for forty years.

She put the bread down.

Then she went back to Holloway's. She went three times over the following two weeks. She tried the Holt Artisan sourdough, the caraway crackers, the sesame rounds, the honey-wheat rolls. She cross-referenced her memory against every small baker she knew of in the region. The caraway crackers were a close derivation of a recipe she'd tasted at a farmers market stall — a woman named June who'd stopped showing up at the market about eighteen months ago. The sesame rounds tasted almost exactly like a bread made by a Lebanese bakery in the Eastport neighborhood that had closed the previous year.

One by one, she mapped them. Six products. Six small bakeries. All of them closed or diminished in the past two years, all of them within the radius that Holt Foods had been actively expanding into.

She laid everything out on the prep counter and stared at it. Bread bags, wrappers, notes on index cards.

"What is all this?" Jamie said, coming in from the back.

"Evidence," Maya said. "Or possibly paranoia. I'm not sure yet."

Jamie picked up the Holt rye bag and read the back. "Heritage grain, slow-fermented, artisan-crafted in our partner facilities. Where do you think the partner facilities are?"

"I think they're food factories in the Central Valley that were handed slightly modified versions of these recipes and told to produce at scale."

Jamie set the bag down. "How do you get someone's recipe?"

"You send someone charming to their shop. You ask about their process. You sample everything. If they're trusting — and most small bakers are trusting because it's a community, we share — they might even show you their method. And if they won't sell their business, you make it hard for them to stay open, and then you don't need them to sell."

Jamie was quiet for a moment. "You think Holt has been doing this deliberately? Targeting small bakeries, extracting recipes, then—"

"Pressuring them out. Then scaling the recipes under his own brand." Maya looked at the array on the counter. "I think I'm next. And I think the reason he wants this specific building, and not just my recipes, is that he wants the story. He wants to say the Holt Artisan Collection started in a real neighborhood bakery with real heritage. He wants *Chen's Hearth* as a brand acquisition."

"He wants your grandmother's story."

The word hit her harder than she expected. She nodded.

"So what do you do?"

Maya started gathering the wrappers into a pile. "I make a phone call," she said. "To a journalist named Priya Nair who already thinks something smells wrong."

She dialed that evening. Priya answered on the second ring, which told Maya she'd been waiting for someone to call.

"I left that comment six weeks ago," Priya said. "I've been watching Holt Foods expand into small-bakery territories for almost two years. I have four former operators who will talk off the record. None of them will go on the record because they're afraid."

"What would it take to go on the record?"

"Something more than suspicion. A pattern with documentation. Or someone willing to be the first name on the story."

Maya looked at the spread of wrappers on her counter. She thought about Andrzej's rye, and June's crackers, and the Lebanese bakery in Eastport, and her grandmother's starter sitting in its crock on the shelf.

"I might be able to give you both," she said.
